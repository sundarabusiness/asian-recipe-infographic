# Beef Pho (Phở Bò)
**Vietnamese Beef Noodle Soup**

Aromatic, deeply savory beef broth poured over rice noodles and thinly sliced beef, finished at the table with a riot of fresh herbs, lime, and condiments. The national dish of Vietnam and one of the world's great noodle soups.

**Serves:** 4 (generous bowls)  
**Active time:** ~45 minutes (plus broth simmering)  
**Broth time:** 3–6 hours (mostly hands-off; make ahead highly recommended)  
**Make-ahead:** Broth keeps 4–5 days in the fridge or months in the freezer.

---

## Ingredients

### Broth
- 3 pounds (1.4 kg) mixed beef soup bones (knuckle, marrow, and/or oxtail — ask your butcher for "pho bones")
- 1 pound (450 g) beef brisket or chuck roast
- 1 large yellow onion, unpeeled, halved
- 1 large piece fresh ginger (about 5 inches / 12–13 cm), unpeeled, halved lengthwise
- 4 star anise pods
- 1 cinnamon stick (3–4 inches)
- 4 whole cloves
- 1 teaspoon coriander seeds
- ½ teaspoon fennel seeds (optional but recommended)
- 8–10 cups (2–2.4 L) water, or enough to cover bones by about 2 inches, plus more as needed during cooking
- 3 tablespoons fish sauce, plus more to taste at the end
- 1 tablespoon rock sugar or light brown sugar, plus more to taste
- 2 teaspoons kosher salt, plus more to taste

### Noodles & Beef for the Bowls
- 12 ounces (340 g) dried flat rice stick noodles (bánh phở, medium or small width)
- 8 ounces (225 g) beef eye of round, sirloin, or tenderloin, sliced paper-thin against the grain (partially freeze 45–60 minutes for easy, clean slices)
- 4–6 ounces (115–170 g) cooked brisket from the broth, thinly sliced (highly recommended for texture contrast)

### Essential Garnish Plate (serve on the side — non-negotiable)
- 3 cups fresh bean sprouts
- 1 large bunch Thai basil (or regular basil + a little mint)
- 1 bunch cilantro (stems and leaves), roughly chopped
- 4 green onions, thinly sliced
- 2–3 limes, cut into wedges
- 2–4 fresh red Thai bird chilies or 1 jalapeño, thinly sliced
- Hoisin sauce
- Sriracha or Vietnamese chili garlic sauce

**Optional add-ins:** beef meatballs (bò viên), tripe, tendon, additional flank or brisket.

---

## Instructions

1. **Parboil the bones (cleaning step).** Place the bones and brisket in a large stockpot or Dutch oven. Cover with cold water. Bring to a rolling boil over high heat. Let boil hard for 5–10 minutes. Drain everything in a colander and rinse the bones and meat very well under cold running water, scrubbing off any gray scum. Wash the pot thoroughly. This step is what keeps your broth clear and clean-tasting.

2. **Char the aromatics.** While the bones are parboiling, char the onion and ginger. Place them cut-side down directly on a gas burner or under a broiler. Turn occasionally until the skins are blackened in spots and the flesh is fragrant (5–10 minutes). Rinse briefly under running water to knock off loose charred flakes — leave most of the blackened parts on for flavor and color. Set aside.

3. **Toast the spices.** In a small dry skillet over medium heat, toast the star anise, cinnamon stick, cloves, coriander seeds, and fennel seeds for 1–2 minutes until very fragrant. Remove from heat. Tie them in a small piece of cheesecloth or place in a large tea ball/infuser so they are easy to fish out later. (You can also just skim them out with a slotted spoon at the end.)

4. **Build and simmer the broth.** Return the cleaned bones and brisket to the clean pot. Add the charred onion and ginger, the spice bundle, and 8–10 cups water (or enough to cover the bones by ~2 inches). Bring to a boil, then immediately reduce heat to the lowest possible simmer — just a few lazy bubbles breaking the surface. Skim any foam or scum that rises during the first hour. Simmer gently, uncovered or with the lid slightly ajar, for at least 3 hours and up to 6 for deeper flavor. Check occasionally and top up with hot water if the bones start to peek above the liquid. In the last hour of simmering, the brisket should be tender; remove it, let it cool slightly, then refrigerate so it is easy to slice thin later.

5. **Strain and season the broth.** Remove and discard the bones, ginger, onion, and spice bundle. Strain the broth through a fine-mesh strainer (or a colander lined with cheesecloth) into a clean pot or large bowl. Skim excess fat from the surface if you prefer a lighter broth (or chill the whole pot overnight — the fat will solidify on top and is easy to lift off). Return the broth to a simmer. Stir in 3 tablespoons fish sauce, 1 tablespoon sugar, and 2 teaspoons salt. Taste. The broth should be fragrant, savory, and slightly sweet, but not aggressively salty — remember it will reduce a bit more and every diner will season their own bowl. Add fish sauce, sugar, or salt in small increments (1 teaspoon at a time) only if needed. Keep the broth at a rolling boil when you are ready to serve.

6. **Prep the garnish plate.** Wash and arrange all the fresh garnishes on a large platter or in individual bowls. This is the fun part — everyone builds their perfect bowl at the table.

7. **Cook the noodles.** Bring a large pot of unsalted water to a boil. Cook the dried rice noodles according to the package directions (typically 3–6 minutes). They should be tender but still have a pleasant chew — do not overcook or they will turn mushy in the hot broth. Drain, give them a quick rinse with cold water to stop the cooking, and divide among 4 deep soup bowls. (Cook the noodles right before serving or cook per bowl to order if you are serving over time.)

8. **Assemble the bowls.** Working quickly while the broth is at a full rolling boil: For each bowl, lay a generous portion of noodles in the bottom. Arrange 5–7 slices of the raw paper-thin beef in a single layer over the noodles (this lets the broth cook it evenly). Add a few slices of the cooked brisket if using. Ladle 2 to 2½ cups of the furiously boiling broth directly over the beef. The heat will instantly cook the thin slices to a perfect rosy medium-rare.

9. **Serve immediately.** Bring the bowls to the table right away with the full garnish plate, hoisin, and sriracha. Diners add big handfuls of bean sprouts and herbs, squeeze lime, scatter chilies and green onion, and swirl in hoisin and chili sauce to their own taste. The contrast of hot savory broth, fresh crunchy vegetables, bright lime, and sweet-spicy condiments is the whole point of phở.

## Vietnamese Tips (Chef's Tips)

These recipes live with the elders, who cook by taste. The numbers below are a safe, recoverable baseline for someone cooking alone. The real flavor happens at the table.

- **Broth clarity and depth come from patience and technique, not from dumping in more fish sauce.** A hard rolling boil during the long simmer will make the broth cloudy and give it off-flavors. Keep it at the gentlest simmer and skim early and often.

- **Char the onion and ginger — do not peel them.** The blackened skins and flesh add natural sweetness and the signature roasted aroma that defines good phở. A quick rinse removes only the loose ash.

- **Slice the beef as thin as possible while it is still semi-frozen.** This is the secret to silky, tender beef that cooks perfectly in the bowl. A sharp knife or even a cheap meat slicer makes this easy.

- **Season the pot conservatively; season the bowl boldly.** 3 tablespoons fish sauce for this volume of broth is intentionally on the mild side. You (and your guests) can always add more fish sauce, a pinch of salt, or a splash of the cooking broth at the table. You cannot take salt out once it is in the pot.

- **The garnish plate is not optional.** The fresh herbs, sprouts, lime, and chilies are what make each bowl alive and personal. Skimp here and you are just eating beef noodle soup, not phở.

- **Make the broth a day (or a month) ahead.** Phở broth improves overnight in the refrigerator. It also freezes beautifully. Reheat gently and boil hard right before ladling so the beef cooks properly in the bowl.

- **Noodles:** Good quality dried Vietnamese or Thai bánh phở is widely available. Fresh noodles (if you can find them) are wonderful — they only need a very brief dip in boiling water.

- **Scaling & leftovers:** This makes four very generous restaurant-style bowls with a little broth left over. The leftover broth is liquid gold for future bowls or even congee. Never freeze a fully assembled bowl — the noodles turn to mush.

Enjoy. This is a dish worth doing properly, and then adjusting exactly to your own palate every time you sit down with it.
